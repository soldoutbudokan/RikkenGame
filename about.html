The game in one minute

Four players, 52 cards, ace high, thirteen tricks. An auction decides who plays which contract: a rik bidder names trump and calls an ace they don't hold — whoever holds that ace is their secret partner, revealed only when the ace hits the table. Higher bids play alone: exactly one trick (piek), zero tricks (misère), nine with your own trump (abondance), zero with your hand face-up (open misère), or all thirteen (solo slim). Scoring is zero-sum: every point someone wins, someone else pays.

Fourth ace (troela) is the one bid you don't choose: dealt exactly three aces, you must declare it (a higher bid can still overcall it). The holder of the fourth ace becomes the declarer's partner — openly, from the start — and names the trump suit from their own hand; the pair needs 8 tricks, scored like a rik. (All four aces: play alone against three and name trump yourself.)

The house rule: the called ace comes out

The first time the called card's suit is played, its holder must play the called card — following to a lead of that suit, or leading the suit themselves, the card must be the called one. Illegal alternatives are simply unclickable, and the AI is bound by the same rule. It makes the declarer's classic probe — lead the called suit early to flush out your partner — actually work.

Why the hands feel juicy: the shuffle

Most card programs shuffle perfectly — a mathematically uniform shuffle of a fresh deck every hand. Real tables don't, and rikken hands live off that difference. This game simulates the whole table cycle:

  1. The pile keeps its history. Completed tricks go onto the pile in the order they were won — and a trick is usually three or four cards of the same suit, because following suit is compulsory. Thrown-in hands (an early-ended misère, an all-passed redeal) land on the pile still sorted, which is even clumpier.
  2. The shuffle is human. Two lazy riffles and a cut. Each riffle splits the pile roughly — not exactly — in half, then interleaves the halves in little packets of one to three cards, the way thumbs actually release them. That detail is load-bearing: with perfect one-card-at-a-time riffles the suit clumps die almost immediately; with packet drops they survive.
  3. The deal concentrates what survived. Cards go out in packets of 4-4-5, so a surviving same-suit run tends to land in one player's hand instead of being sprayed around the table.

Measured over hundreds of simulated cycles against a perfect shuffle: the average longest suit in a hand rises from 4.9 to 5.2 cards, and hands with a seven-card or longer suit more than double, from 4% to 10%. More long suits, more voids, more hands worth bidding — without becoming a farce. The first hand of a session riffles a fresh pack; a single RULES.shuffle setting restores the sterile perfect shuffle if you prefer it.

The AI, honestly

The opponents' card play is rule-based — sound, not brilliant. They take tricks with the cheapest winning card when an enemy is winning, draw trumps when declaring, dump dangerous high cards under a misère declarer, and track what's been played. They don't know who the hidden partner is until the called ace appears — the same fog you play under. Bidding is shared by every level and is done by simulation, not by scoring points for honours: each plausible contract — every trump suit worth naming, every card worth calling — is played out to the end of the hand across dozens of randomly dealt worlds. The bid-or-pass line itself is not guessed either: it was calibrated on tens of thousands of logged self-play auctions with randomized decisions, which measured that passing a bid-worthy hand quietly costs one to two points — so the table bids rather more than looks natural, on purpose. Misère-family bids keep strict low-hand shape rules, and three aces declare the fourth ace as the rules demand.

Three skill settings. Casual plays a completely random legal card 15% of the time and fumbles its discards; sharp never does. Benchmarked head-to-head (two vs two, alternating seats, with control tables to prove the harness fair), the sharp pair beats the casual pair by about half a point per hand.

Hardest stops guessing entirely. Before every card it deals out dozens of complete worlds consistent with everything it can legitimately see — its own cards, every card played, who showed void in which suit, what the auction implies (how long and how strong a rik bidder's trump suit actually runs and how short they sit in the called suit — distributions mined from thousands of self-play deals — plus a misère bidder's provably low hand, that the called card never sits with the declarer, and that nobody who passed a quiet auction holds three aces), the revealed partner — then plays each legal card to the end of the hand in every world and picks the best expected score. It never peeks: unknown cards are re-dealt randomly for every sample. Measured over ~2,400 hands, the hardest pair beats the sharp pair by 0.94 ± 0.13 points per hand — a bigger step than sharp over casual — winning 61% of the hands that move money and 69% of 16-hand sessions, making its contracts 75% of the time to sharp's 64%. Bidding is shared by every level, so that gap is card play alone. Every strength change is measured by an in-repo benchmark (ai-bench/) against the previous version before it ships.

Online play with nobody in the middle

This site is static — there is no server, no account, no database. Online games run peer-to-peer over WebRTC: the host creates an invite code, each guest sends back a reply code (swap them over any chat app), and from then on the game travels directly between browsers.

Privacy is enforced by architecture, not honesty: the host's browser runs the authoritative game and sends each guest a redacted view — other players' cards travel as anonymous backs, and the hidden partner's identity is stripped out entirely. Every action a guest sends is re-validated against the rules on the host's side. A modified client can't see your hand, because your hand never reaches it. If a guest drops, the AI quietly takes over their seat.

One caveat: without a relay server, a small fraction of strict-NAT network pairs can't connect. That's the price of serverless.

Bigger tables

Rikken scales to five or six players the traditional way: everyone stays at the table, but each hand is played by exactly four. With five, the dealer sits out their own deal; with six, the dealer and the player opposite. Sitters neither pay nor receive that hand, and the deal rotates over everyone — so over a full evening it evens out.

Playing from the keyboard

Everything is playable without a mouse, and every button wears its own key: P passes, each bid shows a digit or letter, trump and called-card choices take 1–4, and during play ←/→ pick a card and Enter plays it. Enter also confirms hand-end and pass-the-device screens.

Hot seat without peeking

Sharing one device, every change of human turn drops an opaque curtain — pass the device to… — before the next hand is revealed. Nothing on screen hints at the hidden partnership either: until the called ace appears, the interface shows per-player trick counts only, because even a side total would give the partner away.

For the curious

The whole game — rules engine, AI, and interface — is one React component: Rikken.jsx on GitHub. Every tunable rule (deal pattern, contract values, the house rule, the shuffle) lives in a single RULES object at the top of the file, so a regional variant is a one-place edit. The engine is pure functions, tested with everything from scripted scenarios to thousands of random AI playouts asserting that following suit is enforced and every hand's points sum to zero. And because the hardest AI scores every option before playing, its habits can be mined: BEST-PRACTICES.md is an auto-generated strategy guide — how often it leads trump, when it ducks, and where gut-feel play measurably loses points.
